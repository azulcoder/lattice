## 2. Geothermal drilling — what makes it different (standard engineering; verified)
- vs oil & gas: **higher temperature**, **harder/abrasive, fractured igneous/metamorphic
  rock**, pervasive **lost circulation**, **larger-diameter** wells (to flow high
  steam/two-phase mass rates), and **corrosive** fluids/gases. ROP is lower; bit/tool
  life shorter; tools must be high-temperature-rated.
- **Well construction sequence**: conductor → surface casing → intermediate casing →
  production casing, each cemented (ideally to surface), telescoping down in diameter,
  then a **production liner** (often slotted/perforated, or open hole) across the
  feedzone(s). Casing-to-surface cementing anchors the string against thermal cycling.
- **Casing design / thermal cycling**: heat-up on production and cool-down on
  shut-in/injection cause large thermal expansion/contraction → axial thermal stress and
  buckling risk; full cementing and proper grade/connection selection manage it. This is
  a defining geothermal casing concern absent in most oil/gas wells.
- **Cementing**: thermally stable (often high-temperature, silica-amended) cement;
  lost-circulation zones make getting a good cement sheath hard.
- **Lost circulation**: drilling fluid disappears into fractures — *the* characteristic
  geothermal drilling problem. Managed with lost-circulation material, aerated/water
  drilling, or **drilling "blind"** (accepting no returns) in the production zone.
- **Directional drilling**: multi-well pads (several deviated wells from one location)
  reduce surface footprint and let wells intersect near-vertical fracture/feedzone
  structures — central to modern field development.
- **Logging & instrumentation**: high-temperature temperature/pressure surveys and
  spinner (flow) logs locate and rank feedzones; completion test informs the liner.
- **Economics**: drilling + completion is roughly a third to a half of project capital,
  so drilling efficiency drives the reserves/decline economics (ties to Sanyal).
