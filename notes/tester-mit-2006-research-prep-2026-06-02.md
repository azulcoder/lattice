## §4 — Key Concepts (with formulas)

1. **What EGS is (petrothermal vs hydrothermal).** EGS engineers fracture permeability into hot, low-permeability rock
   by **hydraulic stimulation**, then circulates injected water (injector → stimulated fracture network → producer) to
   mine heat. Hydrothermal systems need heat + fluid + *natural* permeability co-located; EGS supplies the fluid and
   *engineers* the permeability, so it can tap the conductive resource almost anywhere given depth. **EGS is NOT
   dry-steam** — dry-steam (Darajat, Larderello) is natural vapour-dominated hydrothermal; EGS is the engineered
   frontier contrast.
2. **Heat-in-place (resource base).** Volumetric sensible heat: `Q = ρ_r · C_r · V · (T_r − T_0)` (rock density,
   specific heat, accessible volume, temperature above a reference). This is the resource **base**, not the recoverable
   reserve.
3. **Recovery factor.** Only a fraction is recoverable: `Q_rec = R_g · ρ_r · C_r · V_active · (T_r,i − T_r,a)` above an
   abandonment temperature. Sanyal & Butler (2005) modelled ~40% (34-47%) for an idealized stimulated volume above
   ~10⁸ m³; MIT applied **conservative R_g of 2-40% (highlighting 2% and 20%)** → > 200,000 EJ recoverable.
4. **Thermal drawdown.** A circulated EGS reservoir cools over its life as the swept rock gives up heat; reservoir
   lifetime and re-drilling cadence depend on the swept volume and flow rate (the engineered analogue of hydrothermal
   decline).
5. **Cost / learning curve.** Levelized cost falls along a learning curve as cumulative deployment grows; the report's
   supply-curve argument is that targeted R&D (drilling + stimulation) drives EGS down to competitiveness.
6. **Induced seismicity.** Hydraulic stimulation can trigger felt microseismicity — the key environmental/social risk
   for EGS (the later Basel and Pohang cases sharpened this), and a recurring theme of the assessment.
